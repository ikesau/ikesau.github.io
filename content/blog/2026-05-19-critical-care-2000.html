--- title: critical care (2000) tags: - essay ---

Last night, I watched the Star Trek Voyager episode Critical Care. In it, the Doctor is abducted into a hospital that is undersupplied & understaffed. He immediately gets to work helping the sick and lambasting the inequity of the hospital's coverage. The rich and powerful get top quality care; the poor are given beds to die on. It turns out the hospital's resources are allocated by a computer (the Allocator), which calculates scores for each patient based on a consequentialist rubric. If you're the CEO of a fertilizer factory whose death would result in the deaths of hundreds of thousands of people, you get a high score. If you're a warehouse worker whose death would only show up in your mother's thoughts, you get a pat on the back.

The analogue is ambiguous - is the Allocator like private healthcare - humane treatment only for those who can pay? Or is the Allocator like a planned economy - oppressively reductive in its metrification of human worth? The hospital's administrator, Chellick, argues that the system is necessary: prior to the Allocator, health outcomes for his society were worse. His role in the system is to own the fact that public health ethics requires ugly sacrifices.

The Doctor resists the logic of his captors, eventually infecting Chellick with a fatal disease and altering his bio-signature so that the Allocator will place him into the no-coverage tier. It's a pretty crazy move for a digital embodiment of the prime directive x hippocratic oath, but the gambit works, and Chellick promises to reform the system. The Doctor is rescued, but his conscience won't let him off so easily - on what basis were his own unethical actions justified?

I think the episode worked decently well. I'm definitely thinking about it more than most Trek I watch, but in part that's because I don't think it does a very good job at resolving the problems at its heart: what is the right mix of deontology to consequentialism in a healthcare system? If at least some of the former, is it right to intervene in the affairs of societies you don't belong to?

The Doctor's position is clearly that the system is not deontological enough, supported by the cartoonish example of "rich guy getting his third vaccine injection, just to be safe" versus "dear child dying for lack of a single shot"

But

  1. If it's truly the case that the rich guy doesn't need his third shot, then the Allocator is being stupid, and the Doctor should just make the consequentialist argument to fix it
  2. Surely the system deals with harder cases than that. What would the Doctor say if there was only one dose of vaccine left for two patients, one elderly and the other young? What if the Allocator was doing an optimal job and now hundreds of thousands of citizens are at a higher risk of dying because of crop failure?

Ultimately, we don't see if the Doctor's actions improve or worsen health outcomes for the colony (perhaps because doing so would implicitly legitimize the consequentialist framing, perhaps because they ran out of time (as these episodes often do.))

It's a shame, because I think doing so would have really sharpened the discussion had about the episode in other reviews I've read. People could have debated whether or not the Doctor was wrong in deciding for others, or without sufficient evidence; or what the right virtue ethics is. Instead, in most reviews of the episode I've read, people argue about socialism vs capitalism and whether or not Canadians are dying because the waitlists for treatment are too long.

Nevertheless, "deontologist fights for deontology by violating deontology due to consequentialist justification" is a pretty good hook. I just wish they'd hung on it a little more.
